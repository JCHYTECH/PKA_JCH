# VETALYX - Market Evidence Pack v1

## 1. Executive decision

VETALYX should not launch in Europe by simply importing the Chinese panel unchanged.

The commercially defensible path is:

1. keep the DIXUN microarray as the technical base;
2. adapt the allergen panel to European exposure patterns;
3. treat food IgE as a secondary decision-support tool, not as a diagnostic claim;
4. position the rapid test as a screening/triage product only if the allergen mix is redesigned;
5. lead with France as validation market, but build specifications from an EU5 panel architecture from day one.

The reason is simple: European clinical credibility will be won on panel relevance and interpretation quality, not only price or speed.

## 3. External evidence base

### Pet population and market base

FEDIAF Facts & Figures 2025 gives the strongest current pet-population anchor for Europe. The report lists around 89.6M dogs and 108.3M cats in Europe, with EU totals around 69.4M dogs and 81.0M cats. For EU5 target countries, extracted figures include:

| Country | Dogs | Cats |
|---|---:|---:|
| France | 7.6M | 14.9M |
| Germany | 10.5M | 15.7M |
| Italy | 8.77M | 10.24M |
| Spain | 9.46M | 5.95M |
| Belgium | 2.05M | 3.08M |

This supports the internal prioritization: France, Germany, Italy and Spain are all structurally large pet-health markets; Belgium is smaller but useful for fast KOL diffusion and Benelux testing.

### Clinical use of allergy tests

The key clinical distinction:

- environmental allergen testing can be useful after clinical diagnosis of atopic dermatitis, mainly to select allergens for immunotherapy;
- serum IgE does not diagnose canine atopic dermatitis by itself;
- food allergy/adverse food reaction remains an elimination diet plus re-challenge diagnosis.

Guideline evidence:

- BMC Veterinary Research guidelines state that selection of test allergens should be based on the prevalence of allergens in the specific geographical region, and that test interpretation must be combined with history and clinical presentation.
- The same guidance states that there is no accurate commercial allergy test to diagnose canine atopic dermatitis; clinical diagnosis and exclusion of other pruritic diseases are required.
- A 2019 Vet Journal study found saliva food IgA/IgM and serum food IgE were not reliable to diagnose adverse food reactions in dogs; elimination diets remain the reference standard.

Commercial implication:

> VETALYX must sell "allergen sensitization mapping to support clinical management and immunotherapy decisions", not "instant allergy diagnosis".

### Competitive benchmark

The European benchmark is no longer only classical ELISA. The leading competitors increasingly sell an allergology ecosystem.

| Competitor | Position | Evidence | Threat |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nextmune PAX / Artuvetrin | Molecular/macroarray IgE + immunotherapy ecosystem | PAX uses 250-300 spots, extracts and molecular components; selected after testing >1,000 canine sera from Europe; CCD blocking is integrated | Highest scientific benchmark |
| Laboklin PAX | European lab channel with PAX | PAX analyses >200 allergen extracts/components with CCD blocking; 1-6 working days; dog/cat/horse | Strong lab-distribution credibility |
| Heska / Antech ALLERCEPT | IgE receptor technology, long allergy history | Heska AG claims veterinary allergy market activity since 1985 and ALLERCEPT IgE systems for dog/cat/horse | Strong specialist credibility |
| IDEXX | Reference lab and regional panels | IDEXX uses geographically specific environmental panels in the US; EU relevance as a model of regionalization | Installed-base threat |
| Generic Asian rapid tests | Low-cost, fast | Price and availability | Commoditization risk |

Competitive conclusion:

VETALYX cannot beat Nextmune/Heska on deep scientific prestige at launch. It can beat them on a pragmatic niche if it offers:

- fast clinic workflow;
- lower price per decision;
- EU-adapted allergen panels;
- clear clinical interpretation;
- reader-enabled reporting;
- distributor-friendly economics.

## 4. Allergen panel gap: China vs Europe

JCH's concern is correct: an allergen panel built for China can be wrong or incomplete in Europe.

The problem is not that Chinese allergens are "false"; the problem is regional exposure and clinical relevance. A panel can be analytically valid but commercially/clinically weak if it misses the allergens vets expect to see in their geography.

### What the current microarray covers well

Mites:

- Dermatophagoides pteronyssinus;
- Dermatophagoides farinae;
- Dermatophagoides microceras;
- Acarus siro;
- Lepidoglyphus destructor;
- Tyrophagus putrescentiae;
- Glycyphagus domesticus;
- Euroglyphus maynei;
- Blomia tropicalis.

This is a strong base. House dust mites and storage mites are highly relevant in canine atopic dermatitis; Spanish data also support strong mite relevance in Galicia.

Moulds:

- Penicillium chrysogenum;
- Cladosporium herbarum;
- Aspergillus fumigatus;
- Aspergillus niger;
- Alternaria alternata;
- Candida albicans.

Useful, though Candida should be interpreted cautiously as colonization/infection context matters.

Weeds/trees/insects:

- birch, beech, oak, elm, sycamore/plane, willow, poplar/cottonwood, ash, mulberry, horse chestnut;
- ragweed, wormwood, mugwort, Japanese hop, plantain, pigweed, wall pellitory/Parietaria, nettle, sunflower, cocklebur;
- mosquito and flea.

This is a serious start.

### What is missing or under-specified for EU5

High-priority EU gap:

- grasses: Timothy grass, perennial ryegrass, orchard grass/cocksfoot, meadow fescue, Kentucky bluegrass, Bermuda/Cynodon for southern Europe;
- alder and hazel: important early tree pollens in central/northern Europe;
- olive: important in Spain, Italy and Mediterranean France;
- cypress/Cupressaceae: relevant in Mediterranean winter/spring allergy;
- Parietaria must be explicitly confirmed as Parietaria judaica/officinalis, not only a generic "wall pellitory" label;
- flea saliva/Ctenocephalides should be explicit, not only "flea";
- Culicoides is relevant if horse/equine expansion is later considered;
- CCD blocking or CCD marker needs to be added or documented.

Medium-priority EU gap:

- Malassezia pachydermatis as a dermatology-associated allergen/marker context, depending on technology and claim;
- house dust mixes relevant to local indoor exposure;
- regional moulds beyond the current core;
- animal epithelia beyond cat/dog if clinically justified.

### Rapid test gap

The current inhalant rapid test is not a Europe-wide screen. It should not be the launch hero in its present form.

Current mix:

- mugwort;
- Candida albicans;
- Penicillium chrysogenum;
- cockroach;
- Dermatophagoides farinae.

Missing from rapid mix:

- D. pteronyssinus;
- storage mites;
- grass pollen;
- birch/alder/hazel group;
- flea;
- olive/cypress for South Europe;
- Parietaria for Mediterranean regions.

Recommendation:

Create EU rapid panels as group screens rather than isolated 5-allergen tests:

| Rapid panel | Proposed content | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| EU Environmental Screen | mite mix, storage mite mix, grass mix, tree mix, weed mix, mould mix, flea/insect | first-line triage |
| France/Benelux Add-on | birch/alder/hazel, grasses, mites, flea, moulds | FR/BE launch |
| Mediterranean Add-on | olive, cypress, Parietaria, grasses, mites, ragweed/mugwort | IT/ES/south FR |
| Germany/Central Add-on | birch/alder/hazel, grasses, mugwort/ragweed, mites/storage mites, moulds | DE launch |

If DIXUN cannot reformulate rapid tests, VETALYX should launch rapid only as a limited demonstrator and lead commercially with microarray + reader.

## 5. Product specification recommendations

### Minimum EU launch specification

Before commercial launch, request from DIAHO/DIXUN:

1. full allergen list by SKU, scientific names, extract source, supplier/source country, lot controls;
2. analytical sensitivity/specificity, CV, repeatability, inter-lot variation;
3. species validation for dog and cat separately;
4. clinical correlation study or retrospective sera set, ideally EU sera;
5. cut-off definition and grey-zone handling;
6. CCD blocking method or evidence of how plant-food/pollen cross-reactivity is controlled;
7. interference testing: hemolysis, lipemia, icterus;
8. sample type validation: serum/plasma/whole blood if claimed;
9. reader calibration and software validation;
10. report template with interpretive warnings.

### Product architecture

Recommended portfolio:

| Tier | Product | Launch priority | Notes |
|---|---|---:|---|
| Tier 1 | EU Environmental Microarray Dog/Cat | P0 | Main credible product |
| Tier 2 | EU Environmental Rapid Screen | P1 | Only after reformulation |
| Tier 3 | Food Sensitization Panel | P2 | "Elimination diet support", not diagnosis |
| Tier 4 | DX Reader + reporting software | P0 | Strategic lock-in |
| Tier 5 | Nutraceutical/skin-barrier add-ons | P3 | After clinical trust is established |

### Claims matrix

Allowed:

- detects allergen-specific IgE sensitization;
- supports clinical management of atopic dermatitis;
- helps identify candidate allergens for avoidance or immunotherapy discussion;
- professional veterinary use only;
- results must be interpreted with history, clinical signs and differential diagnosis.

Avoid:

- diagnoses allergy alone;
- diagnoses food allergy;
- detects intolerance;
- replaces elimination diet;
- predicts clinical reaction to every food;
- universal European panel without regional qualification.

Preferred wording:

> VETALYX maps allergen-specific IgE sensitization in dogs and cats to support veterinary decision-making in suspected allergic disease, especially environmental atopy and immunotherapy planning.

## 7. Financial and commercial implications

The internal France revenue model can stand as a base case only if three conditions are met:

1. the panel is perceived as clinically relevant by French dermatology/KOL clinics;
2. the report prevents misuse and protects against food-allergy overclaim;
3. the distributor sees a low-support, low-complaint product.

Otherwise the likely failure mode is not regulatory rejection but slow adoption and reputation drag.

Commercial recommendation:

- start with 5-10 KOL clinics, but do not ask only "does the test work?";
- ask KOLs to score each allergen as must-have, useful, optional, irrelevant for their region;
- build a France/Belgium panel first, then Mediterranean and Germany variants;
- measure repeat-order behavior, not one-time sample acceptance.
